# JK Architect of the Year (AYA) 2026: Green Architecture Award
## Project: Safal Vihaan

**Location:** Sanathal, Ahmedabad, Gujarat, India
**Category:** Green Architecture Award
**Status:** Completed (2024-2025)

---

### 1. Project Overview
Safal Vihaan is a landmark residential project in Ahmedabad that redefines the relationship between high-density housing and the natural environment. Built within a mature grove of over 500 existing chikoo (sapodilla) trees, the project demonstrates a radical commitment to environmental stewardship, where the architecture was sculpted to preserve the existing ecosystem rather than replace it.

### 2. Design Philosophy: Architecture as Stewardship
The design philosophy, led by Ar. Jagrut Patel in collaboration with Kamal Mangaldas and Devendra Shah, centers on the concept of "stewardship rather than dominance." Instead of clearing the site, the buildings weave through the dense orchard, creating a series of courtyards and interstitial gardens that blur the boundaries between indoor and outdoor living. This approach ensures that every residential unit has a direct, intimate connection with the preserved landscape.

### 3. Sustainability and Microclimate
The preservation of the mature tree canopy is the project's primary sustainable strategy. The trees provide:
- **Natural Solar Shading:** Significantly reducing incident solar heat gain on the building facades.
- **Evaporative Cooling:** Creating a microclimate several degrees cooler than the surrounding urban environment.
- **Carbon Sequestration:** Maintaining the site's role as a local carbon sink.

The architecture employs passive design principles, including deep-set balconies and high-performance glazing, to maximize natural light while minimizing thermal gain. The use of natural materials and earthy tones ensures the buildings recede into the landscape, allowing the ecosystem to remain the primary focus.

### 4. Social Impact and Innovation
Safal Vihaan offers a unique, park-like living experience. The preservation of existing trees has created an immediate sense of "place" and history, fostering a deep sense of ownership and well-being among residents. The project serves as a powerful case study for Indian urbanism, proving that the protection of natural assets is a catalyst for, rather than a constraint on, architectural innovation.
